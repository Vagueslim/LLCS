LCCS
Why What Nodes Flows Research
01 — Why this exists
"The people saving lives
shouldn't have to
work until they break."

Every time a disaster happens in Thailand, the same images appear. Officials sleeping at the site. Staff writing notes on paper. Three people coordinating a hundred volunteers by phone, surviving on instant noodles and no sleep.

The problem is not that these people aren't capable. The problem is that there is no system to hold the information for them. So it all lives in their heads. And when they finally go to sleep, the knowledge goes with them — and the next shift starts from zero.

LCCS started as a question: what if the system remembered, so the humans didn't have to?

What if a commander could hand off a shift and the next person could walk in, read a screen, and know exactly what's happening — without a 40-minute briefing?

What if a volunteer arriving at the site could be registered, assigned, and deployed in 3 minutes instead of standing in a confused crowd?

What if a family looking for their mother could just search a name — and get an answer — instead of calling 12 different hotlines?

LCCS is that system. Not to replace the humans. To give them rest.

02 — What LCCS is
01
A command layer
LCCS sits above the chaos. It doesn't do the rescuing. It holds the map, the status, the resources, and the decisions — so the people doing the work don't have to hold it all in their heads.
02
An operations backbone
11 nodes, each covering a real function that happens at every disaster site: command, field ops, inventory, medical triage, survivor shelter, food, sanitation, communication, and people search.
03
Designed for degraded conditions
Disasters break infrastructure. LCCS works on Starlink, LoRa mesh radio, and offline-first mobile apps. If the internet dies, the system keeps running and syncs when connection returns.
03 — Where the idea comes from
The image that started it
Tham Luang, 2018. The cave rescue that Thailand watched for 17 days. Officials on-site around the clock. Volunteers from 20 countries. A governor sleeping in a tent. Resources pouring in with no coordinated intake. It worked — because extraordinary people did extraordinary things under impossible pressure. But it shouldn't require that. It shouldn't cost that much of a person.
The research
While designing LCCS from scratch, I discovered that the US has been running the Incident Command System (ICS) since 1970, and MIT Lincoln Laboratory built NICS — an open-source command platform used in 40+ countries. The Sahana Foundation built disaster management software after the 2004 Tsunami. What I had designed independently aligned with all of them. That meant the problem was real. And the gap — a system designed for Thai context, Thai language, Thai infrastructure — was also real.
What LCCS adds
ICS defines the structure. NICS provides the platform. But neither solves the last-mile problem: a volunteer who just arrived with no credentials, a family searching for a missing person in Thai, a field team reporting from a zone with no internet. LCCS is designed for those three people specifically.
04 — The 11 nodes
Each node is a real function that happens at every disaster site. Most of them are currently handled by one exhausted person with a phone and a notebook. Click any node to see why it exists, what it does, and what happens if it breaks.

N-03
Operation Teams
Field
+
The hands. The only node that physically touches survivors. Every other node exists to support this one. If Ops fails, nothing else matters.
The real problem it solves
Field teams work disconnected from command — in rubble, in water, without signal. They need mission cards that work offline, and a way to report back that doesn't require a phone call. LCCS gives them a form that syncs when signal returns, and a mission card that tells them exactly where to go and what to bring.

N-04
Inventory & Equipment
Logistics
+
The stockroom. Three asset types: things you consume, things you borrow and return, things you use once. Without this, supplies run out silently and equipment disappears.
The real problem it solves
At Tham Luang, volunteers counted supplies manually. Nobody knew how much food was left until it was almost gone. This node gives every downstream node — kitchen, medical, field — a live count of what's available and warns before it hits zero.

N-05
Power & Comms Infra
Critical
+
The one node that, if it fails, takes everything else down with it. Dedicated team, 24h. Not optional.
The real problem it solves
Power and comms are always buried inside logistics — handled by whoever is around. That means when the generator dies at 2am, there's no dedicated team to fix it. LCCS makes this a named node with its own staff, its own fuel tracking, and its own redundancy plan.
Infrastructure
Generator + Fuel Starlink (LEO satellite) VHF Radio LoRa Mesh (offline) Offline-First Sync
Dependency note
Single point of failure. All 10 other nodes depend on this one. Redundancy is not optional — it is the entire design requirement of this node.

People Registry
Public
+
The most human node. Status: safe, hospitalized, in shelter, missing. Anyone can search. No account needed. No phone call required.

N-10
Sanitation & Welfare
Often Missed
+
The forgotten node. Toilets, clean water, waste management. By day 3 without this, disease spreads faster than any rescue team can handle. It is never in the initial plan. It should be.
The real problem it solves
Sanitation is always added reactively — after the first person gets sick. LCCS puts it in the Day 1 setup checklist, not the Day 4 emergency list. Also houses psychosocial support: by Day 3, both survivors and responders are showing signs of trauma that need dedicated attention — not a triage nurse with 40 other patients.

06 — Research & alignment
ICS
Incident Command System — US, 1970
The standard for emergency command structure worldwide. LCCS mirrors its hierarchy: Command → Operations → Logistics → Medical. Developed after a catastrophic miscommunication during California wildfires.
NICS
Next-Generation Incident Command System — MIT Lincoln Lab
Open-source web platform used by 40+ countries. Originally built for wildfire response. LCCS extends this concept with Thai-language support, offline-first mobile, and last-mile logistics tracking.
INSARAG
International Search and Rescue Advisory Group — UN
Sets global standards for Urban Search & Rescue teams. The building marking system (triage cross on walls) and the two-wave search protocol (hasty + grid) in LCCS come directly from INSARAG guidelines.
LCCS was designed independently — starting from watching the Tham Luang rescue and asking why it had to be so hard — before discovering that ICS, NICS, and INSARAG already existed. The fact that the design converged with global standards without referencing them is the most useful finding of this project. It suggests the problems LCCS addresses are universal, not Thai-specific. The gap it fills — Thai language, Thai infrastructure, last-mile field operations, volunteer identity — remains real and unaddressed by existing tools.
